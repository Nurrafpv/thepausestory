THE
PAUSE

One breath, taken before the answer, can change everything that comes after.

✦ a five-year lesson, told in panels ✦
Hi All — apologies for taking a while to put this together. I wanted to write it properly… because this one sits close to me.

It looks simple on the surface. But the moment I really understood its power—it stayed.

…tick… tick…
1 THE SESSION
Rewind five years. Orientation week. A session led by Shikhar…
SHIKHAR When we're young, curious, and eager to prove ourselves—the second we know an answer, our instinct is to fire it back.
SHIKHAR But before you give that answer… can you pause? Is this really the only way to look at the question?
…PAUSE.
before the answer leaves your mouth

Most of the time there is a straightforward answer. But sometimes that small pause cracks open a completely different possibility—an approach nobody tested, a perspective that wasn't obvious at first.

It won't always hit the jackpot… but it gives me the chance to look past the most immediate answer.
2 YES vs NO
Over time I saw the same principle everywhere—especially in how we handle work, opportunities, and responsibility.

A new task lands. And most of us see it in pure black and white…

YES!

Said instantly—so the door never closes. Even without the bandwidth. Even out of fear of being passed over next time.

VS

NO.

Said instantly—to protect time, energy, work-life balance. The reason may be valid… but it lands as a full refusal.

…but wait.

Apply the pause again, and you realize—the best answer usually isn't at either extreme.

IT LIVES IN THE GREY.

somewhere between the two.

3 THE TASK
Picture it. Someone asks: “Can you finish this by tomorrow?”
REFLEX A “Yes, I'll do it!” (all ten steps… somehow.)
REFLEX B “No, I need more time.”
…PAUSE…
The task has ten steps. So instead of overpromising all ten—or refusing outright…

deliver 6–7 • share the direction • keep the door open
THE BETTER ANSWER “By tomorrow I may not finish all of it—but I'll complete six or seven steps and share the initial direction.”
THAT CHANGES
EVERYTHING.
not overpromising • not denying • still moving

You give context. You show ownership. You create progress. I've seen it in my own work—and with my mentees. The difference is rarely yes or no. It's whether we paused and said what's realistically possible.

4 THE CORE WORK
And it runs straight into the real work.
Have one Tier 1? → Can I still find a second solution?
No perfect Tier 1? → Can I build a stronger position—combinations, legal arguments, alternate interpretations?
One path dead? → Can I still find the smaller dots that strengthen the case?

It all comes back to one question:

Are we pausing…
or grabbing the closest answer?
A direct YES or a direct NO
may not always work.

The real value lives in the space between—and that space only opens when you bring the full context. It keeps the open, willing part of “yes,” and the honest, no-overcommitting part of “no.”

One small pause…
before you answer.
